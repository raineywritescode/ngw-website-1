Don't let the US government hack our computers.

The update to Rule 41 seeks to create a new avenue for U.S. government agents to break into our computers, take data, and use other malicious hacking techniques.

It especially targets people using privacy-protective technologies, including Tor and VPNs.

The United States Congress never approved this new expansion of powers. But now Congress is our last chance to stop this change from taking effect.

What’s wrong with the update to Rule 41?

  • It creates a lower legal threshold to search or hack into the computers of people using privacy protective technologies. This means those most concerned about privacy are at the greatest risk for government overreach.

  • Malicious hacking—stealthily breaking into computers, copying data, deleting data, or executing code—is a highly dangerous tool to use. The government should have strict policies on when such extreme measures are taken, balancing the privacy impacts of others who may be impacted as well as how to handle data which may be inadvertently accessed.Right now, no such rules exist.

  • Congress has never considered—much less debated—how and when magistrate judges should issue these types of sweeping search warrants, which would effectively allow law enforcement agents to utilize malicious hacking techniques. This would be a vast expansion of powers with no public debate or consideration.

  • This update to Rule 41 will allow magistrate judges to sign off on warrants for computers all over the world, not just in the United States, regardless of the legal protections baked into the laws of other countries.

  • A government agent could actually do more damage to a computer during a botnet investigation than the botnet itself.

  • A government agent could forum shop, finding the most prosecution-friendly or technically-naïve magistrate in the United States to sign off on these hacking search warrants.
